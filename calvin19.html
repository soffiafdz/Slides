UK BioBank Papers

NIST Lab

Sofía Fernández Lozano

Purpose

  1. Assess whether any associations between baseline cognitive and incident dementia may be accounted for by constitutional/modifiable risk factors;
  2. and, whether cognitive scores add anything to prediction models of the disease.

Sample (UKBB)

  • 502,617 (age: 40-69; across UK; 2006-2010)
  • 488,363 (blood samples for genotyping)
  • 1,051 cases of incident dementia
    • 352 AD
    • 169 VaD
  • 385,336 (591 dementia) main analytic sample
  • 65,824 (112 dementia) genetic sample
  • 130,212 (115 dementia) all-cognitive sample

Ascertainment of incident dementia

Dementia was identified from:

  • ICD (9&10) codes of hospital inpatient admission data (1996 - Feb 2016)
  • ICD10 codes on death certificates (Apr 2006 - Feb 2016)

*Cases were selected if the date of first recorded Dx occurred at least three years after baseline assessment

Assessment of cognitive function

Brief computer-administered cognitive battery of four test for fluid cognitive abilities:

  • Processing speed (reaction time task)
  • Visual episodic memory (visual pairs memory task)
  • Prospective memory (prospective memory task)
  • Fluid & crystallized abilities (verbal-numerical reasoning task)

Models

  1. Univariate age- and sex-adjusted logistic regression models (cognitive, constitutional and modifiable risk factors)
  2. Model1 - age, sex, education and 1/4 cognitive tests
  3. Model2 - added constitutional risk factors
  4. Model3 - added modifiable risk factors
  5. *Models were repeated to include participants with missing data on covariates with >5% missingness

Association of risk factors for dementia

Prediction by baseline cognitive performance

  • Effect sizes for total dementia risk according to reaction time and visual memory were similar for the main sample and the sample with incomplete cases
  • No obvious difference between OR for dementia D× within three to five years of cognitive testing and those made six to eight years later

Adjustment for risk factors

  • This was conducted on all-cause dementia
  • Adjusting for risk factors didn't affect the associations between cognitive tasks and dementia risk, which remained statistically significant in fully-adjusted models

Discriminative accuracy of models

with and without cognitive variables

Comparison to previous studies

  • Reproduced ROC curves adding the four cognitive tests to a basic model including age, sex, and education (N=160,903; 177)
  • AUC went from 0.763 (95% CI: 0.729, 0.796) to 0.821 (0.790, 0.853), χ2 = 31.9, P <.001

Strenghts

  • Large scale of cohort & rich data set
  • Age range covering mid to late adulthood

Limitations

  • Referral bias
  • Electronic health linkage without primary care data may have led to underdetection of dementia
  • Low-to-moderate test-retest reliability of cognitive tests
  • Missing data from participants with lower cognitive ability and/or poorer health were likely to have reduced sensitivity due to selective attrition

Main conclusions

  • Even a brief cognitive test battery has predictive power at the population level
  • AD was defined by a relatively stronger association with APOE4 and VaD risk by slower processing speed and greater vascular risk factors
  • None of the modifiable risk factors but depression increased risk for AD; several did for VaD
  • Modifiable risk factors had almost no impact on the performance of the prediction models that included cognitive and genetic factors
